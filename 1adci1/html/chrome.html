    Who is Chia? Chia is a contemporary artist in the early 21st century. She inspired the creation of chiado. What is Chiado? In the Naruto universe, nindo (literally "Ninja Way") is a personal creed, motto, or philosophy that a shinobi lives by. It serves as a moral compass that guides a character's actions, decisions, and growth throughout their life. Chiado (literally “Chia Way”) is a real-life application of this concept. Chiado is a heterodox, henotheistic, dystheistic mystical nindo practiced only by Kenny. Chia is perceived as gaining divinity through apotheosis. Divinity in this context is described as an imperfect pursuit for optimization of ontological efficacy. Divinity is in the pursuit itself rather than a perfect ethereal endpoint. There is no need for unfalsifiable claims of omniscence in this context. In a mereological nihilistic context, religion is hypothesized to be a social technology. God is a functional psychological and sociological construct, possibly in part, as an effect of overactive agency detection. Atheistic awe produces a different neural signature than religious awe. Chiado posits that some aspects of religious awe can be garnered through the apotheosis of Chia without sacrificing epistemological rigor, by constructing chiado as part of an emerging social media milieu that is subverting some aspects of religion in the current zeitgeist of the information age (circa early 21st century). In some ways, chiado is like an open label placebo for spirituality. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qGGG0hnitQM Henotheism In Luciferianism, the Lucifer archetype represents the pursuit of knowledge, enlightenment, and individual sovereignty — embodying the defiance of ignorance and dogma in favor of rational self-mastery and human transcendence. Chia doesn’t represent the Lucifer archetype per se, but she embodies the techniques to obtain such an archetype through molding chakra. Chakra elements In the Naruto universe, chakra is the fundamental source of life energy that all living beings possess. It functions as the magical power system of the universe, allowing ninja to perform superhuman feats like walking on water, creating clones, or breathing fire. In chiado, chakra represents various aspects of psychology and physiology that affect cognition, behavior and physicality. The 5 elements of chakra. Earth: Check your physical existence and observe the physical world around you with your senses. Wind: Intellect, alertness, movement and speed. Fire: Passion, energy and endurance. Water: Fluid, feelings, emotions, flexibility and sound therapy. Ether: Astral projection, higher consciousness or into the void. Chia techniques This part isn’t finished yet but it will have the techniques used to mold chakra. Types of human perception 1. Interoception This is the sense of the internal state of the body. It involves various receptors in our internal organs, bones, and skin that send signals to the brain to help regulate functions like hunger, thirst, and temperature. Nociception: The perception of pain. Thermoception: The ability to sense heat and cold. Baroception: The sensing of blood pressure. Chemoreception: Detection of internal chemical changes, such as blood oxygen levels or carbon dioxide. 2. Proprioception Often called the "sixth sense," proprioception is the awareness of the position and movement of the body. It relies on receptors in the muscles, tendons, and joints. Kinesthesia: Specifically refers to the awareness of the motion of our limbs and body parts. Vestibular Sense: Originating in the inner ear, this provides our sense of balance and spatial orientation, helping us understand which way is "up." 3. Exteroception These are the senses that perceive the external environment. Beyond the traditional five, scientists often break these down further based on the stimuli they detect: Vision (Ophthalmoception): Perception of light and color. Hearing (Audioception): Perception of sound vibrations. Taste (Gustaoception): Detection of chemical flavors. Smell (Olfacception): Detection of airborne chemical molecules. Touch (Somatosensation): A complex system that includes pressure, vibration, and texture. 4 Chronoception How we perceive the passage of time. This isn't tied to a single organ but is a distributed cognitive process. 5 Agency The subjective awareness that you are the one initiating and executing an action. The major networks Default mode network (DMN) — anchored in the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), and angular gyrus. It's most active when you're not focused on an external task: daydreaming, imagining the future, recalling the past, thinking about yourself or others. It's the brain's "resting state" that's actually quite active. Salience network — centered on the anterior insula and dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC). Its job is to detect what matters right now — biologically significant, emotionally charged, or unexpected stimuli — and switch the brain between the DMN and task-focused networks. It's essentially a gatekeeper. Dorsal attention network (DAN) — built around the frontal eye fields (FEF) and intraparietal sulcus (IPS). This is top-down, voluntary attention: deliberately directing your gaze or focus to a location or object. It works with the task-positive network. Ventral attention network (VAN) — centered on the temporoparietal junction (TPJ) and ventral frontal cortex. This is bottom-up, reflexive attention: your head snapping toward a sudden loud noise. It breaks into the dorsal system to reorient when something important demands attention. Task positive network — the broad set of regions (dlPFC, ACC, parietal cortex) that collectively ramp up during any effortful, externally directed task. It's the rough anti-correlation partner to the DMN — when one is up, the other tends to be down. Frontoparietal control network — the dlPFC and lateral parietal cortex working together for flexible, goal-directed control. It bridges the salience network and the DMN/task-positive systems, helping you adapt strategies and regulate other networks. Cingulo-opercular network — dACC, anterior insula, and thalamus. Maintains sustained task sets over time, monitors for errors, and keeps you "online" during prolonged tasks. Often considered the slower, more tonic counterpart to the faster frontoparietal network. Anatomically constrained systems Sensorimotor, visual, auditory, limbic, and language networks are more anatomically constrained systems handling their respective modalities. Metacognition: where is it and how does it monitor? Metacognition is the brain's ability to think about its own thinking — monitoring confidence, detecting errors, evaluating memory, and modeling one's own mental states. It doesn't have a single dedicated area; instead it emerges from a distributed set of nodes that are embedded within other networks, allowing second-order monitoring. Key regions and what they monitor: Rostrolateral/anterior PFC (BA10) — the most "metacognitive" area. Active when you hold a mental task in mind while doing another (branching), and during prospective memory. It monitors ongoing cognition at a high level. mPFC — overlaps heavily with the DMN. Crucial for judging one's own beliefs, values, and mental states. It monitors the self-model. dlPFC — monitors working memory and task performance. Signals when something isn't going as planned. Overlaps with the task-positive and frontoparietal networks. Anterior insula — interoceptive metacognition: monitoring your own bodily and emotional states ("I feel uncertain"). Bridges salience and metacognitive functions. Temporoparietal junction (TPJ) — tracks the difference between your own mental states and others'. Key for agency (is this action mine?) and perspective-taking. Overlaps with the ventral attention network. Precuneus / posterior cingulate cortex — autobiographical self-monitoring, episodic recall about yourself, and scene construction. Deeply embedded in the DMN. dACC — error detection and performance monitoring. Fires when outcomes mismatch expectations, feeding that signal upward to metacognitive evaluation. Key insights The key insight is that metacognition doesn't watch the brain from outside — its nodes are the same nodes that run the DMN, salience, and frontoparietal networks, just recruited into second-order loops. When you monitor your own confidence in a memory, the hippocampus and PCC run the memory, and the mPFC/anterior PFC evaluates how reliable that memory feels.
